**Penicillin V and amoxicillin are both first-line antibiotics** for GAS pharyngitis in adults and children, with **no clinically significant difference in efficacy** between them. **Cephalosporins are not first-line agents** but are reserved for penicillin-allergic patients. **Standard treatment duration is 10 days** to prevent acute rheumatic fever. **Amoxicillin/clavulanate is not indicated** for uncomplicated GAS pharyngitis and offers no advantage over plain amoxicillin.[1][5][20][30]

**First-Line Antibiotic Choice**

The **IDSA 2012 guidelines** recommend **penicillin or amoxicillin** as first-line therapy based on narrow spectrum, proven efficacy, safety, low cost, and absence of resistance (GAS remains uniformly susceptible to penicillin).[1][20][30][31] The **AHA guidelines** also endorse once-daily amoxicillin as first-line therapy.[1][16] Both agents are equally effective; amoxicillin may offer improved compliance with once-daily dosing.[11][36]

**Cephalosporins are not first-line agents.** While some data suggest cephalosporins may have marginally lower rates of treatment failure (NNT 20), this benefit is inconsistent across analyses and does not justify routine use of broader-spectrum, more expensive antibiotics.[2][13][22] A 2023 Cochrane review concluded that low-certainty evidence supports penicillin as first choice in the context of antimicrobial stewardship.[2][22]

**Standard Treatment Duration**

**10 days of treatment** is recommended to maximize pharyngeal eradication of GAS and prevent acute rheumatic fever.[9][11][20][30][32] This duration has been the standard since the 1950s based on studies demonstrating prevention of rheumatic fever.[12] While shorter courses (3-6 days) of newer antibiotics may be effective in high-income settings where rheumatic fever is rare, the 10-day regimen remains standard, particularly in populations at higher risk for rheumatic fever.[8]

**Penicillin-Allergic Patients**

Management depends on the **type of hypersensitivity reaction**:[1][6][15][16][19]

- **Type IV (delayed) hypersensitivity**: First-generation cephalosporins (e.g., cephalexin 20 mg/kg/dose twice daily, maximum 500 mg/dose) for 10 days[6][15][28]

- **Type I (immediate/anaphylactic) hypersensitivity**: Avoid all beta-lactams. Options include:
  - **Clindamycin** 20 mg/kg/day in 3 doses (maximum 900 mg/day) for 10 days[6][15][28]
  - **Azithromycin** or **clarithromycin** (macrolides) - though **significant resistance exists** in many U.S. regions[5][6][15][18][21]

**Important**: The **2024 AAFP guidelines** recommend obtaining **throat culture and sensitivity testing** if prescribing non-beta-lactam antibiotics due to increasing resistance to azithromycin, clarithromycin, and clindamycin.[5][18][21]

**Amoxicillin/Clavulanate (Augmentin)**

**Amoxicillin/clavulanate is NOT indicated** for uncomplicated GAS pharyngitis.[20][26] The addition of clavulanate (a beta-lactamase inhibitor) provides no benefit since GAS does not produce beta-lactamase and remains uniformly susceptible to penicillin.[31][34]

The **IDSA 2012 guidelines** list amoxicillin/clavulanate only as an option for **treatment of chronic GAS carriers** (40 mg amoxicillin/kg/day in 3 doses for 10 days), not for acute pharyngitis.[20][26] Using amoxicillin/clavulanate for routine GAS pharyngitis represents unnecessary broad-spectrum coverage, increases cost, and may cause more gastrointestinal side effects.

**Key Supporting Evidence**

The 2023 Cochrane review of 19,162 participants found no clinically important differences between antibiotic classes compared with penicillin, supporting penicillin as first choice.[2][22] GAS has never developed documented resistance to penicillin despite decades of use.[31][34] The modest symptom benefit of antibiotics (NNT 6 at 3 days, NNT 21 at 1 week) is primarily for symptom reduction and prevention of suppurative complications and rheumatic fever, not cure of a self-limited illness.[9][32]
