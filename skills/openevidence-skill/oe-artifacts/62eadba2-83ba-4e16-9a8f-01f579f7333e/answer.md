The American Academy of Family Physicians recommends using the **TiTrATE approach** (Timing, Triggers, and Targeted Examination) to systematically evaluate adults presenting with dizziness, as patient descriptions of symptom quality are unreliable for establishing a diagnosis[1][4].

**Timing-Based Classification**

The first step is to categorize patients by **timing** into two major syndromes[1][4]:

- **Episodic vestibular syndrome**: Intermittent episodes of dizziness or vertigo
- **Acute vestibular syndrome**: Continuous, persistent dizziness or vertigo lasting days

**Trigger Assessment**

After establishing timing, determine whether symptoms are **triggered or spontaneous**[10]:

- **Triggered episodic**: Provoked by specific actions or movements (e.g., positional changes in benign paroxysmal positional vertigo, standing in orthostatic hypotension)
- **Spontaneous episodic**: No clear trigger (e.g., Meniere disease with hearing loss, vestibular migraine with headache history, panic attacks with psychiatric symptoms)
- **Triggered acute**: Usually caused by medications, toxins, or trauma (traumatic brain injury, tympanic membrane rupture, vertebral artery dissection)
- **Spontaneous acute**: Includes vestibular neuritis, transient ischemic attack, or stroke

**Targeted Examination**

The physical examination is tailored to the timing and trigger pattern[13]:

- **Triggered episodic syndrome**: Perform Dix-Hallpike maneuver (transient upbeat-torsional nystagmus suggests benign paroxysmal positional vertigo) and orthostatic vital signs (sustained reduction of ≥20 mm Hg systolic or ≥10 mm Hg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing)
- **Spontaneous acute syndrome with central concern**: Perform HINTS examination (head-impulse, nystagmus, test of skew), which has 97% sensitivity and 96% specificity for identifying central causes like stroke when performed by trained clinicians[13]

**Red Flags for Central or Dangerous Etiologies**

Critical misconceptions to avoid when evaluating for stroke[19]:

- **Do not assume** "true vertigo" implies a peripheral cause—focus on timing and triggers rather than symptom quality
- **Do not assume** hearing loss or tinnitus implies a peripheral cause—rule out central causes first
- **Do not assume** headache with dizziness is vestibular migraine—consider vascular pathologies including aneurysm
- **Do not assume** strokes must present with limb ataxia or focal signs—HINTS examination findings are more sensitive and specific than focal neurologic signs
- **Do not assume** young patients have migraine rather than stroke—consider vertebral artery dissection
- **Recognize imaging limitations**: CT has poor sensitivity for posterior fossa stroke; even diffusion-weighted MRI can be falsely negative and may require repeat testing in 48 hours

**Key Associated Symptoms to Elicit**

For **spontaneous episodic syndrome**, associated symptoms guide diagnosis[10]:

- Hearing loss, tinnitus, or ear pressure → Meniere disease
- Migraine history → Vestibular migraine  
- Psychiatric symptoms → Panic attack or psychosis

For **acute vestibular syndrome**, obtain medication and toxic exposure history, as numerous medications can cause dizziness[10]. Assess for trauma history including head injury, whiplash, and skull fracture.

**Differential Diagnosis**

Common peripheral causes include benign paroxysmal positional vertigo, vestibular neuritis, and Meniere disease. Central causes requiring urgent evaluation include vertebrobasilar ischemia, stroke, intracranial hemorrhage, meningitis, encephalitis, cerebellopontine angle tumors, craniocervical dissection, multiple sclerosis, and seizures. Other serious causes include acute coronary syndrome, aortic dissection, arrhythmia, and pulmonary embolism[12][17].

**Additional Evidence on History-Taking Approach**

The traditional approach of categorizing dizziness by symptom quality (vertigo, presyncope, disequilibrium, lightheadedness) is **outdated and flawed** because patients have difficulty accurately describing their symptoms[3][9][11]. The timing-and-triggers paradigm is evidence-based and more reliably distinguishes benign from dangerous causes[11].

**Specific History Questions by Category**

For patients with **acute vestibular syndrome** (continuous symptoms lasting days), key questions include[20]:

- **Vascular risk factors**: Hypertension, diabetes, atrial fibrillation, prior stroke—these increase stroke likelihood
- **"Deadly Ds"**: Diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, dysphonia, dysmetria, dysesthesia—any suggest central etiology
- **Ability to stand independently**: Inability to stand unaided despite peripheral-appearing eye findings warrants MRI to confirm or exclude stroke

For **episodic symptoms**, clarify[8][9]:

- **Onset and duration**: Seconds (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo), 20 minutes to 24 hours (Meniere disease), variable (vestibular migraine)
- **Specific positional triggers**: Rolling over in bed, looking up, bending forward
- **Orthostatic triggers**: Symptoms when moving from sitting/supine to standing
- **Auditory symptoms**: Unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus, aural fullness
- **Headache characteristics**: Migraine features (photophobia, phonophobia, aura)
- **Autonomic symptoms**: Nausea, vomiting, diaphoresis
- **Cardiac symptoms**: Palpitations, chest pain, dyspnea

**Medication Review**

Obtain a comprehensive medication history, as many drugs cause dizziness including antihypertensives, diuretics, sedatives, anticonvulsants, and ototoxic agents[10]. Recent medication changes or toxic exposures are particularly important in triggered acute vestibular syndrome.

**Head Trauma and Psychiatric Context**

Specifically ask about recent head trauma, whiplash injury, or ear trauma (barotrauma, tympanic membrane rupture)[10]. For spontaneous episodic symptoms without clear vestibular features, explore psychiatric history including anxiety, depression, panic disorder, and hyperventilation syndrome[8][10].
