The **Wurtman-Fernstrom tryptophan/LNAA hypothesis has limited support in human studies with normal mixed meals**, while **emerging evidence favors alternative mechanisms including vagal afferent activation, glucose-sensing by sleep-promoting neurons, and orexin/hypocretin inhibition** as primary drivers of postprandial somnolence.

**Tryptophan/LNAA Hypothesis: Evidence and Limitations**

The classical hypothesis proposes that carbohydrate-induced insulin secretion promotes muscle uptake of branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs: leucine, isoleucine, valine), increasing the plasma tryptophan:LNAA ratio and facilitating tryptophan transport across the blood-brain barrier, thereby increasing brain serotonin synthesis[2][4]. Wurtman et al. (2003) demonstrated in 9 healthy subjects that carbohydrate-rich breakfasts (69.9g carbohydrate, 5.2g protein) versus protein-rich breakfasts (15.4g carbohydrate, 46.8g protein) produced significantly different plasma tryptophan:LNAA ratios (median difference 54%, range 36-88%, P<0.01), with insulin rising after carbohydrate but not protein meals[3]. The mechanism operates through insulin-mediated selective uptake of LNAAs by muscle, while tryptophan levels remain stable, creating competitive advantage for brain uptake[2].

However, **critical limitations** undermine this hypothesis for real-world postprandial somnolence. As little as 5% protein content in a meal prevents the increase in tryptophan concentrations, making the mechanism unrealistic for typical mixed meals[1]. Additionally, increased serotonin does not necessarily translate to melatonin production, which is regulated by darkness rather than meal timing[1]. Only 2-3% of dietary tryptophan (approximately 0.5g daily in Western diets) is used for central serotonin production[2].

**Vagal Afferent Pathway: Recent Mechanistic Evidence**

A 2025 study by Chen et al. provides direct circuit-level evidence for vagal mediation of postprandial sleep. In male mice, activation of stomach/duodenum-innervating vagal sensory neurons reduced wakefulness and increased NREM sleep, while chemogenetic inhibition abolished feeding-induced sleep promotion[6]. Using anterograde transsynaptic tracing and single-nucleus RNA sequencing, investigators identified a specific pathway: upper gut vagal afferents → GABAergic neurons in the nucleus of solitary tract (NTS) → paraventricular nucleus of hypothalamus (PVH). Optogenetic activation of NTS GABAergic neurons and their PVH projections suppressed wakefulness and prolonged NREM sleep[6]. This represents the first direct demonstration of the neural circuitry driving postprandial sleep.

**Glucose-Sensing Mechanisms**

Varin et al. (2015) demonstrated that glucose directly excites sleep-promoting neurons in the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO) of mice. Glucose infusion into the VLPO promoted slow-wave sleep and increased c-Fos expression selectively in VLPO neurons. Patch-clamp recordings showed that VLPO neurons with sleep-promoting properties are selectively excited by glucose within physiological range through glucose catabolism leading to closure of ATP-sensitive potassium (K_ATP) channels[9]. This mechanism provides direct metabolic state sensing by sleep centers.

**Orexin/Hypocretin Inhibition**

Hypocretin/orexin neurons in the lateral hypothalamus promote wakefulness and arousal, with defects causing narcolepsy[10]. These neurons alter their intrinsic electrical activity according to ambient nutrient levels and appetite-regulating hormones, representing strong candidates for neural correlates of postprandial sleepiness[10]. The glucose-induced excitation of VLPO sleep-promoting neurons likely works in concert with orexin neuron inhibition during fed states.

**Gut Hormone Mechanisms**

Wells et al. (1997) conducted paired studies in 18 healthy volunteers comparing high-fat-low-carbohydrate versus low-fat-high-carbohydrate morning meals. Cholecystokinin (CCK) concentrations were significantly higher after high-fat meals, while insulin was higher after high-carbohydrate meals. Subjects reported greater sleepiness 2-3 hours after high-fat-low-carbohydrate meals, with significantly greater fatigue ratings at 3 hours, consistent with CCK-mediated lassitude[8]. This suggests CCK may contribute independently of the tryptophan mechanism.

**Alternative Hypotheses**

Bazar et al. (2004) challenged the traditional "blood redistribution" myth, noting that cerebral perfusion is preferentially maintained during diverse physiologic states (e.g., exercise) and that no measurable change occurs in common carotid artery blood flow postprandially[7]. They proposed that postprandial release of gut-brain hormones (melatonin, orexins) and vagal activation modulate hypothalamic sleep centers[7].

The inflammatory hypothesis suggests high-glycemic-index diets stimulate inflammatory immune responses (IL-1β, IL-6) that may affect sleep quality[1], though direct mechanistic studies linking postprandial cytokines to acute somnolence are lacking in the retrieved literature.

**Synthesis**

Current evidence suggests postprandial somnolence results from **multiple parallel mechanisms** rather than a single pathway. The vagal afferent pathway provides the most direct circuit-level evidence, while glucose sensing by VLPO neurons and orexin inhibition offer complementary metabolic signaling. The tryptophan/LNAA hypothesis, while biochemically sound for pure carbohydrate loads, appears insufficient to explain somnolence after typical mixed meals containing protein. CCK release after fat ingestion represents an additional hormonal mechanism. The relative contribution of each pathway likely varies with meal composition, timing, and individual metabolic state.
