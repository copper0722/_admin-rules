**Differential diagnosis** of severe hyperglycemia (>600 mg/dL) distinguishes between **DKA, HHS, and simple severe hyperglycemia** based on the presence of ketosis and acidosis. **DKA** requires hyperglycemia (typically >250 mg/dL, though euglycemic DKA exists), pH <7.3, bicarbonate <15-18 mEq/L, and positive ketones (beta-hydroxybutyrate >3.0 mmol/L). **HHS** presents with more severe hyperglycemia (>600 mg/dL), pH >7.3, bicarbonate >15 mEq/L, minimal or absent ketones, and hyperosmolality. **Simple severe hyperglycemia** lacks both significant ketosis and acidosis[1][2][4].

**Guideline-recommended insulin dosing** from the **ADA 2024 consensus** and current standards favors **continuous IV insulin infusion at 0.1 units/kg/hr without an initial bolus** for adults with DKA and HHS. The **JBDS-IP guidelines** recommend a **lower fixed-rate infusion of 0.05 units/kg/hr** for HHS, increasing by 1 unit/hr as needed[7][8][9][12]. A **fixed 30-unit IV bolus is not appropriate** and is not recommended by current guidelines, which have moved away from bolus dosing in favor of continuous infusion started 1-2 hours after fluid resuscitation[9][11][14].

**Risks of large insulin boluses** include **hypoglycemia, severe hypokalemia** from rapid intracellular potassium shift, and potentially **cerebral edema** (particularly in pediatric populations, though the mechanism remains debated). Current protocols emphasize starting insulin infusion only after adequate fluid resuscitation and ensuring potassium is >3.3 mEq/L before initiating insulin to mitigate hypokalemia risk[9][13][14].

## Diagnostic Criteria

The diagnostic criteria differentiate these conditions based on specific laboratory parameters[1][2][4]:

**DKA (all criteria must be met):**
- Glucose >250 mg/dL (euglycemic DKA: <200 mg/dL with known diabetes)
- pH <7.3 (arterial or venous)
- Bicarbonate <15-18 mEq/L
- Anion gap >10-12 mEq/L
- Positive urine or serum ketones
- Beta-hydroxybutyrate >3.0 mmol/L

**HHS:**
- Glucose >600 mg/dL
- pH >7.3
- Bicarbonate >15 mEq/L
- Trace or negative ketones
- Serum osmolality typically >320 mOsm/kg
- Often altered mental status or coma

**Simple severe hyperglycemia:**
- Glucose >600 mg/dL
- No significant acidosis (pH >7.3)
- No significant ketosis
- Normal or mildly elevated anion gap

Approximately 10% of DKA cases present as euglycemic DKA (glucose <200 mg/dL), often associated with SGLT2 inhibitor use, pregnancy, reduced food intake, or alcohol use[1][7]. Mixed DKA-HHS presentations occur in approximately 10% of cases and carry higher mortality than either condition alone[1][7].

## Insulin Dosing Recommendations

**ADA 2024 Consensus and Current Standards (2026):**

For **DKA in adults**[7][9][11][14]:
- **No initial IV bolus** is recommended
- Start **continuous IV regular insulin infusion at 0.1 units/kg/hr**
- Begin insulin **1-2 hours after initiating fluid resuscitation**
- Ensure potassium >3.3 mEq/L before starting insulin
- Target glucose reduction of ~50 mg/dL/hr and bicarbonate increase of ~3 mEq/L/hr
- When glucose reaches 250 mg/dL, **decrease infusion to 0.05 units/kg/hr** and add dextrose to IV fluids
- Continue insulin infusion until DKA resolution (pH >7.3, bicarbonate ≥18 mEq/L, anion gap normalized)

For **HHS**[7][12]:
- **ADA/Diabetes Canada**: 0.1 units/kg/hr fixed-rate infusion
- **JBDS-IP**: 0.05 units/kg/hr, increasing by 1 unit/hr if targets not met
- Lower initial rates reflect concerns about rapid osmolality changes

**Alternative subcutaneous protocols** for mild-to-moderate DKA[9][11][18]:
- Rapid-acting insulin (lispro/aspart): 0.3 units/kg bolus, then 0.1 units/kg hourly
- Can be used in emergency department or step-down units
- Requires aggressive fluid management and frequent monitoring
- May reduce ICU admissions and nursing workload

**Transition to subcutaneous insulin**[9][11]:
- Administer basal insulin (glargine) 2-4 hours before stopping IV insulin
- Continue IV insulin for 30-60 minutes after first subcutaneous rapid-acting dose
- Early administration of basal insulin (0.2-0.3 units/kg) during IV infusion may prevent rebound hyperglycemia

## Risks of Large Insulin Boluses

**A fixed 30-unit IV bolus is inappropriate** because it:

**1. Causes severe hypoglycemia**[9][13][14]:
- Excessive insulin drives glucose down too rapidly
- Risk increases when glucose reaches 250 mg/dL but ketoacidosis persists
- Current protocols mandate decreasing insulin to 0.05 units/kg/hr at glucose 250 mg/dL specifically to prevent hypoglycemia

**2. Precipitates dangerous hypokalemia**[9][13][14]:
- Insulin drives potassium intracellularly, causing rapid serum potassium decline
- Patients with DKA/HHS have total body potassium depletion despite initially normal or elevated serum levels
- Large boluses cause precipitous drops in serum potassium
- Guidelines require potassium >3.3 mEq/L before starting insulin and mandate holding insulin if potassium falls below normal range
- Potassium monitoring every 2 hours (hourly if abnormal) is standard during treatment
- Hypokalemia risk is why insulin is decreased to 0.05 units/kg/hr when glucose reaches 250 mg/dL

**3. May increase cerebral edema risk**[13]:
- Cerebral edema is a rare but serious complication, particularly in pediatric DKA
- Rapid osmolality changes from aggressive insulin and fluid therapy may contribute
- Mechanism remains debated but includes rapid glucose correction causing osmotic shifts
- Current protocols emphasize gradual correction with target glucose reduction of ~50 mg/dL/hr

**4. Does not improve outcomes**[9][11][15]:
- Historical practice included 0.1 units/kg IV bolus followed by infusion
- Current evidence shows **no benefit to bolus dosing**
- Continuous infusion without bolus is now standard practice
- Fixed-rate infusion provides more predictable, controlled correction

## Management Principles

**Fluid resuscitation is the priority**[1][7][14]:
- Begin with 15-20 mL/kg/hr or 1-1.5 L/hr of 0.9% normal saline
- JBDS recommends replacing ~50% of estimated fluid deficit within first 12 hours
- Insulin should be delayed 1-2 hours after starting fluids

**Electrolyte monitoring and replacement**[9][13]:
- Add potassium to IV fluids once levels are below upper limit of normal and urine output established
- Monitor potassium every 2 hours (hourly if abnormal)
- Bicarbonate not routinely recommended
- Phosphorus and magnesium replacement only for severe deficiencies

**Resolution criteria for DKA**[1][9]:
- pH >7.3
- Bicarbonate ≥18 mEq/L
- Anion gap normalized
- Glucose <200 mg/dL
- Clinical improvement
